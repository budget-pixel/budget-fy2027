Walton County Community Profile Walton County is in the northwestern part of Florida, a vibrant and historically rich region known for its stunning natural beauty and charming coastal communities. Established in 1824 from portions of Escambia County, it was named after Colonel George Walton, Jr., Secretary of West Florida Territory and Acting Governor of West Florida from 1821 to 1822. The area’s diverse history begins with the Native American tribes, including the Choctaw and Creek, and continued with 19th-century settlers who helped shape Florida’s timber and lumber industry. John L. McKinnon, in his History of Walton County , described its setting with eloquence: “Lying here, as it were, in the protecting arms of her bounding rivers, with these loving daughters keeping faithful watch, with her head resting in the lap of Alabama that says, ‘Here let us rest,’ while the great Mexican Gulf, at one time, humbly washes her feet with its gentle waves to make them pure and white like snow.” Walton County offers a blend of relaxation, outdoor adventure, and cultural experiences that make it a truly special place to visit and call home. Walton County's History Walton County Heritage Association Walton County Florida Bicentennial Annual Comprehensive Financial Report The Annual Comprehensive Financial Report (ACFR) is an extensive, audited financial statement that provides a detailed and transparent overview of Walton County's financial activities and expenditures over the past fiscal year. Prepared for the financially oriented reader, the ACFR offers an in-depth examination of how public funds are allocated and utilized, ensuring a clear understanding of the county's financial health and management practices. With nearly two decades of ACFRs available online, these reports serve as a valuable resource for those seeking a comprehensive analysis of Walton County's fiscal policies and spending. Link to Annual Comprehensive Financial Report Walton County Interactive Mapping Population and People AAccording to the 2020 Census, Walton County’s population grew by 36.8%, from 55,043 in 2010 to 75,305, largely due to new residents moving in from other parts of the U.S. The largest age group is 55 to 59, while the smallest is 85 and over, giving Walton a median age of 44.6—older than Florida’s median of 42.8. Veterans make up 11% of Walton County’s population, compared to 7.4% statewide. In neighboring Okaloosa County, the median age is younger at 38.6, the veteran population is higher at 20.5%, and its population structure is anchored more heavily by younger adults, particularly those in their late 20s, reflecting a contrast to Walton County’s older age profile and retiree appeal. Income In Walton County, families have a median income of $92,596, with married-couple families earning significantly more at $114,666, while nonfamily households earn $51,930. Overall, Walton County’s median household income is $74,629, slightly below Florida’s state median of $77,735. In comparison, neighboring Okaloosa County reports a higher median household income of $82,291, with families earning $96,567, married-couple families at $108,720, and nonfamily households at $52,989, underscoring generally stronger household earnings relative to Walton County. Education In Walton County, among residents aged 25 and older, 24.3% hold a high school diploma or equivalent as their highest education level, 24.0% have some college but no degree, 11.8% an associate’s degree, 20.6% a bachelor’s degree, and 14.5% a graduate or professional degree—meaning that 35.1% have a bachelor’s degree or higher. In terms of school enrollment, about 77.8% of people aged 3 and over are in kindergarten through 12th grade, 8.7% are enrolled in undergraduate college, and 10.0% are in graduate or professional programs. Comparatively, in Florida, 35.8% of adults 25+ hold a bachelor’s degree or higher (2024 ACS estimate). In Okaloosa County, the share of adults with a bachelor’s degree or more is about 34.2% (5-year ACS estimate) , and 47.11% of its population has at least an associate’s degree plus beyond (i.e. associate or higher) with 93.3% holding a high school diploma or higher. Employment In Walton County, 66.1% of workers are employed by private companies, 10.3% are self-employed in their own incorporated businesses, 4.7% work for private not-for-profit organizations, 12.8% are employed by local, state, or federal government, and 6.2% are self-employed in unincorporated businesses or unpaid family roles. Statewide in Florida, about 83.3% of civilians are private wage and salary workers, 7.3% are government workers, and 9.0% are self-employed (not incorporated) (with unpaid family workers making up a small share). For Okaloosa County, detailed breakdowns by class of worker are available from Census table S2409. The trends show a similar pattern to the state, with a majority employed in private sector roles, followed by government and self-employment categories. Housing The housing landscape in Walton County is quite varied: about 5.5% of homes are valued at less than $50,000, 3.0% fall between $50,000 and $99,999, 7.4% between $100,000 and $149,999, 6.6% between $150,000 and $199,999, 9.9%between $200,000 and $299,999, 24.1% between $300,000 and $499,999, 28.1% between $500,000 and $999,999, and 15.4% are worth $1,000,000 or more. According to the 2020 Census, of the county’s 29,981 housing units, 26,216 are occupied and 3,765 are vacant, yielding a homeownership rate of 74.9%. The median gross rent in Walton is $1,462. In Florida, the homeownership rate was 68.4% in 2024. Among renters across the state, median rent has climbed sharply—rising from about $1,238 to $1,719 in recent years. In nearby Okaloosa County, the homeownership rate is 67.8%, and the median property value is approximately $324,800. Median rent in Okaloosa is harder to pin down precisely, but historical data shows median gross rent of $1,150in 2019, and more recent listings suggest median monthly rents near $1,995 in 2025. Business and Economy In Walton County, a significant portion of the local economy is tied to tourism and related industries, though the employment distribution is more diversified. Among the civilian workforce age 16 and over, 19.0% work in professional, scientific, management, administrative, and waste management services; 15.1% in education, health care, and social assistance; 11.2% in retail trade; 10.9% in arts, entertainment, accommodation, and food services; 9.9% in finance, real estate, rental, and leasing; 9.8% in construction; 7.9% in public administration; 6.8% in transportation, warehousing, and utilities; 4.0% in other services (excluding public administration); and 3.2% in manufacturing. Comparatively, in Okaloosa County, trade, transportation, and utilities account for 16.9% of employment, and government accounts for 19.4%. Additionally, accommodation & food services and retail are among the leading employment sectors locally.
