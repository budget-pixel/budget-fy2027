Introduction and Overview

Transmittal Letter

To the Honorable Walton County Board of County Commissioners: In accordance with Florida Statute Chapter 129.03(3), I am transmitting the Fiscal Year 2026-2027 Tentative Budget for consideration by the Board of County Commissioners. The budget is balanced and establishes the operating framework necessary to provide County services for the forthcoming fiscal year. The budget has been prepared with a County Operating millage rate of 3.519 mills and a North Walton Mosquito Control District millage rate of 0.441 mills. The Fiscal Year 2026 countywide taxable value was $46,594,067,031. Based on a projected 4% increase in taxable value, the estimated Fiscal Year 2027 countywide taxable value is $48,457,829,712. At the current County Operating millage rate of 3.519 mills, the calculated rollback rate for Fiscal Year 2027 is approximately 3.384 mills. The proposed budget was developed with the following objectives: Maintaining existing service levels for residents and visitors Funding the Sheriff, Constitutional Officers, courts, and other statutory obligations Supporting infrastructure improvements, capital projects, and maintenance of County facilities and assets Investing in employee recruitment, retention, compensation, and workforce stability Maintaining a financially sound self-insurance program for employee health insurance Preserving appropriate fund balance reserves, including a minimum unassigned General Fund emergency reserve equal to 20% of annual General Fund operating expenditures The FY 2026-2027 Tentative Budget includes funding for county operations, public safety, infrastructure and capital improvements, transportation, constitutional officers, debt service obligations, and state-required programs and allocations. Additional detail regarding revenues, expenditures, staffing, and major operating funds is provided in the following sections of this document. The Human Services activity category has increased 12%, driven by increased funding for the DeFuniak Community Redevelopment Agency, and an additional funding request from the Walton County Health Department to maintain current primary care services within Walton County. Data Updated: Jun 12, 2026, 1:46 PM As demonstrated in Graph 2 below, General Government Taxes remain the county's largest revenue source, accounting for 77% of budgeted revenues and projected at $254 million, with Ad Valorem taxes making up 66% or $167 million of the total. The projection for Ad Valorem taxes is supported by an increase in the County-Wide Certified Taxable Value, rising from $42.5 billion in fiscal year 2025 to $46.6 billion in fiscal year 2026, a 9.6% increase. The Discretionary Sales Surtax now contributes 15.5% or $39.7 million to be utilized primarily for solid waste collection and disposal, as well as funding road and bridge construction and maintenance. Intergovernmental Revenues, expected to rise slightly to $29 million, include all revenues received from federal and state government sources. These revenues come in the form of grants, shared revenues, and payments in lieu of taxes. This category encompasses the half-cent sales tax as well as other state revenue-sharing streams, making it a critical component of the county’s budget. Miscellaneous Revenues are anticipated to increase 37% to $14.8 million, primarily from increased budgeted interest earnings and an increase in expected Recreation Plat Fee collections. Charges for Services are projected to generate $14 million, reflecting a 5% increase from the prior year’s budget, in line with the sustained demand for county services. Permits, Fees, and Special Assessments are budgeted at $3.2 million, a decline from the prior year. Opportunities and Challenges in Budget Development As we move through the budget process and into the upcoming fiscal year, we are aware that there are several factors to consider that could potentially impact government operations over the next year. There is a possible impact on revenues due to legislative changes related to sales and property taxes. Another long-term concern is the uncertainty of future economic conditions. While tourist development taxes are collected and used within that fund for expenditures allowed under Florida Statute related to tourism, sales taxes recognized in Solid Waste and General Funds are impacted by tourism levels within the County. The budget development process presents both challenges and opportunities that will strengthen our financial planning and resource allocation for the future. As we move through the remainder of FY 25 and into the next fiscal year, it will be imperative to closely monitor those and other revenue sources that the County relies on to balance the budget. The county’s reliance on sales tax revenue is particularly significant, given that a large portion of ad valorem taxes are allocated to the budgets of constitutional officers. This reliance makes it even more critical to manage our resources effectively. The half-cent and one-cent surtaxes, though projected to remain moderately flat this year, offer us a unique opportunity to be more strategic and efficient with our existing resources. By focusing on optimizing our financial management practices, we can continue to support essential services and infrastructure projects without compromising the quality of life for our residents. Similarly, although tourist development taxes are expected to experience a decline from recent years, this is a natural rebalancing as global travel has normalized post-pandemic. The dip offers us the chance to refine our tourism strategies and focus on attracting visitors who will contribute positively to the local economy. Overall, while there are challenges ahead, they also offer opportunities for growth, innovation, and improved financial management. By tackling these challenges with a proactive and strategic approach, we can ensure that our community remains resilient and well-prepared for the future. Full-Time Equivalent Position (FTE) Summary The tentative budget includes a net increase of 17.5 full-time equivalent positions (FTEs), consisting of 13.5 FTEs under the Board of County Commissioners, 3 FTEs for the Clerk of Courts, and 1 FTE for the Sheriff’s Office. A detailed personnel summary is included within each departmental budget section within this document. Proposed staffing additions include: Beach Operations: 7 FTEs Beach Tram Operations: 6 FTEs Emergency Management: 0.5 FTE Clerk of Courts: 3 FTEs Sheriff’s Office: 1 FTE The tentative budget also includes the elimination of 1 FTE within Engineering Services. The staffing changes included in the tentative budget are intended to support operational service demands and maintain organizational capacity in key service areas. The majority of proposed additions are concentrated within Beach Operations and Beach Tram services, reflecting continued operational and workload requirements.
