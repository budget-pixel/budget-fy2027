Summary of Revenues

Revenue Overview Introduction Walton County’s revenue structure is comprised of a diverse range of funding sources that support public services, infrastructure, public safety, and long-term capital investment. The following section provides an overview of the County’s primary revenue classifications and the major funding sources within each category. Revenue projections are developed using a combination of historical trend analysis, economic forecasting, state-shared revenue estimates, and information provided by the Florida Office of Economic and Demographic Research. Additional consideration is given to local development activity, tourism trends, fuel consumption patterns, and broader economic conditions that may impact County revenues. Together, these revenue sources form the financial foundation that supports County operations and strategic priorities. This analysis provides a summary of the County’s fiscal structure while illustrating the diversity and stability of revenues used to fund services for residents and visitors throughout Walton County. Revenue sources within this category include Ad Valorem Taxes, Tourist Development Taxes, Sales Surtax, and Local Option Fuel Taxes. Ad Valorem Taxes Ad valorem taxes are the largest source of revenue for Walton County and result from the levy of taxes on real property and tangible personal property. Preparation of the proposed ad valorem tax budget utilizes the Property Appraiser's final taxable values, which are typically finalized on the DR-420 (Certificate of Taxable Value) form on July 1. Millage rates are charged against the taxable value to arrive at the total tax on each parcel. One mill equals a $1 tax for each $1,000 of taxable value. Ad Valorem Tax revenues for Walton County have consistently increased, reflecting rising property values and effective property tax assessments. The projection for Ad Valorem taxes is supported by an increase in the County-Wide Certified Taxable Value, rising from $42.5 billion in fiscal year 2025 to $46.5 billion in fiscal year 2026, a 9% increase. In accordance with statutory budgeting practices, the County budgets only 95% of anticipated Ad Valorem tax collections, providing a conservative estimate that accounts for potential delinquencies and collection variances. Data Updated: Jun 12, 2026, 1:46 PM Tourist Development Tax Walton County's Tourist Development Tax (TDT) were established in accordance with Florida Statute 125.0104 . The Walton County Tourism Department and its related divisions are funded by these taxes collected from visitors on short-term lodging rentals within two distinct TDT districts. The South Walton TDT district collects 5% on short-term rental accommodations located south of the Intracoastal Waterway, while the North Walton TDT district collects 3% on short-term rental accommodations located north of the Intracoastal Waterway. Tourist Development Tax revenues have seen growth in prior years, resulting from increased tourism. It is projected to remain flat for fiscal year 2026, which is a natural rebalancing as global travel normalizes post-pandemic. This funding source also provides a significant portion of funding for capital projects. The tax is ongoing and does not have a sunset clause. While revenues from the surtax experienced steady growth over many years, collections have remained relatively flat in recent fiscal years, reflecting a stabilization in consumer spending patterns and economic activity that impacts sales tax revenue. Despite this leveling off, the surtax continues to be a critical source of funding for essential county services and infrastructure investments. Data Updated: Jun 12, 2026, 1:46 PM Local Option Fuel Tax In Walton County, the local option fuel tax, established under Florida Statute 206.41(1)(e) and applied according to Statute 336.025, finances essential transportation and infrastructure projects. The tax is set at five cents per gallon for motor fuel and diesel fuel, with an additional one-cent tax known as the "ninth cent." These funds are used for maintaining and repairing county roads and bridges, enhancing traffic safety, and improving public transportation. This revenue ensures that transportation remains safe, reliable, and efficient for both residents and visitors, supporting the county's growth and development. According to Florida Statute 336.025, counties can impose this local option fuel tax for up to ten years, meaning it will need to be renewed in 2028 to continue its benefits. The Local Option Fuel Tax and Ninth Cent Tax have maintained a relatively stable revenue stream, with minor fluctuations. This stability underscores consistent fuel consumption and reliable tax collection efforts. Major revenue sources within this category include the Local Government Half-Cent Sales Tax, State Fuel Taxes, and State Revenue Sharing distributions used to support County operations, transportation, and public service programs. Local Government Half-Cent Sales Tax In Florida, the local government half-cent sales tax program, established under Florida Statute 218.61, is a key revenue source for municipalities and counties. This program allocates a half-cent of the state's sales tax to local governments, supporting infrastructure projects, public safety, parks, and other essential services. The funds, distributed monthly, enable local governments to enhance community services and facilities, fostering improved quality of life across the state. The Local Government 1/2 Cent Sales Tax has shown steady growth over the years, with significant peaks during the summer months reflecting strong economic activity. It is projected to remain flat in fiscal year 2025-26 due to inflationary pressure and stabilized consumer spending. Data Updated: Jun 12, 2026, 1:46 PM State Fuel Taxes These taxes are collected by the state and remitted to the county. The Constitutional Fuel Tax, outlined in Florida Statute §206.41(1)(a), imposes a 2-cent-per-gallon tax for road construction and maintenance, distributed based on a formula. The County Fuel Tax, under Florida Statute §206.60, allows for up to a 1-cent-per-gallon tax for county transportation projects, with distribution based on fuel consumption. The Municipal Fuel Tax, as per Florida Statute §206.605, imposes a 1-cent-per-gallon tax for municipal road maintenance, distributed based on population. If a municipality lacks the capacity to manage transportation projects, it may pass a resolution authorizing the Florida Department of Transportation or the county (if the county agrees and has the capacity) to perform or oversee the engineering and construction of those projects. Fuel tax revenues have been relatively stable, with slight increases year-over-year. This stability indicates consistent fuel consumption. An allocation formula is used to distribute 2.9 percent of net cigarette tax collections and 2.044 percent of sales and use tax collections among qualifying counties. This formula considers each county's total population, unincorporated population, and sales tax collections to determine its share. State Revenue Share Proceeds have generally increased over time, but the most recent year showed a slight decline. This suggests that revenues may be stabilizing, reflecting a potential plateau in the underlying economic activity that drives these distributions. Data Updated: Jun 12, 2026, 1:46 PM Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher Program Walton County receives federal funding through the Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) Program, administered by the Walton County Housing Agency under contract with the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). These funds support rental assistance for eligible low-income families and help cover administrative functions such as application review, eligibility verification, housing inspections, and ongoing compliance monitoring. The program is operated under an Annual Contributions Contract with HUD, with all revenue restricted to eligible housing activities defined by federal regulations. This funding plays a key role in expanding access to affordable housing in Walton County and supporting the day-to-day operations of the local Housing Agency. Annual revenues fluctuate based on federal appropriations and local demand. Sheriff Resource Officers In accordance with the contractual agreements between the Walton County Sheriff’s Office and the Walton County School District, funds are received from the District to support the Sheriff’s Office in providing School Resource Officers (SROs) at schools throughout the county. This revenue ensures that the Sheriff’s Office can fulfill its obligations under the agreement, maintaining trained personnel and necessary resources to promote a safe and secure learning environment for students and staff. Revenue Classification Charges for Services The Charges for Services classification reflects revenues generated from charges for current services provided by the County. This category includes revenues associated with services performed for private individuals, businesses, and other governmental units. Major revenue sources within this classification include planning fees, permitting-related revenues, and MSBU fees. Planning Fees Planning fees are essential for supporting our county's development processes, including zoning applications, site plan reviews, and land use changes. These fees fund the Planning Department's operations, ensuring timely application processing, professional reviews, and high service standards. They help maintain infrastructure, promote smart growth, and enhance community engagement. This revenue stream is vital for sustainable development and maintaining our residents' quality of life. Planning fees have remained relatively stable; however, the introduction of short-term rental certification has caused this category to increase starting in fiscal year 2024. Data Updated: Jun 12, 2026, 1:46 PM Fire Rescue MSBU The Walton County Fire Rescue Municipal Service Benefit Unit (MSBU) is a vital funding mechanism established to support fire rescue services, facilities, and programs throughout the county. The assessment rates for residential properties are set at $75.00 per dwelling unit, while non-residential properties are charged based on their usage category: $0.12 per square foot for commercial, $0.01 for industrial/warehouse, and $0.12 for institutional properties. Land properties, such as agricultural or vacant land, are assessed at a rate of $0.07 per acre. The Fire Rescue MSBU revenues have remained stable, showing a steady collection of assessments for fire rescue services.
